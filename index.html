Third-party candidate says North Shore would benefit from K-12 education reform

By Jack Walker

TK TK, 2025

For some Staten Island voters, casting city council ballots this year might not be as simple as voting red or blue. Sarah Blas, a community advocate with a background in public health, is running to represent the North Shore under two additional tickets — one as an independent, and another under the progressive Working Families Party.

On Nov. 4, Blas will face Democratic incumbent Kamillah Hanks and Republican challenger John Shea in the election for New York City Council’s 49th district. The district spans most of Staten Island north of Interstate 278, including the neighborhoods of Stapleton, West New Brighton, Port Richmond and St. George.

Hanks defeated Blas in the Democratic primary earlier this year, winning more than 58% of the vote. Blas finished in third at about 15%, and said it was difficult to defeat an incumbent in the crowded primary race. But Blas said her desire to represent the North Shore pushed her toward a third-party campaign, and that supporting K-12 education is a cornerstone of her candidacy.

Blas spoke with NYCity News Service about how she would address K-12 education as a councilmember. NYCity News Service has compiled her stances on four key issues: chronic absenteeism, public school enrollment, school funding and universal after-school care.

Election Day

Election Day for New York City is Nov. 4. Residents across the five boroughs can vote on representatives for their city council districts, plus a slate of other offices — including public advocate, borough president, city comptroller and, most notably, mayor.

For this story, NYCity News Service also contacted Hanks and Shea to ask how they would approach each of these issues in K-12 education. Hanks did not return phone call or email requests for comment. Shea did not respond to multiple email requests for comment, and has no publicly listed phone number for his campaign.

New Yorkers ages 18 and up who have lived in the city for at least 30 days prior to the election are eligible to vote, with some exceptions. For more information on the election and registering to vote, visit the city’s Board of Elections website.
